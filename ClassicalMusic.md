## Era Differences

Classical eras differ in texture (layers), harmony (chords/progressions), rhythm (drive), melody (shape), dynamics (volume contrast), and emotional focus—evolving from ornate machinery to raw personal expression. Baroque feels polyphonic and propulsive like a riff machine; Classical is balanced like a pop hook; Romantic swells emotionally; Modern jars with dissonance; Post-modern loops hypnotically.

### Baroque (1600–1750)

Dense polyphony (multiple melodies weaving), basso continuo (bass + chords), steady motor rhythms, terraced dynamics (sudden shifts), ornate trills/sequences, continuous motion evoking grandeur or devotion. Harmony is functional but urgent; no big crescendo. Example: **Bach - Toccata and Fugue in D Minor (organ)** – Opens with dramatic runs, then fugue layers chase the theme relentlessly, building tension through imitation not volume.

### Classical (1750–1820)

Homophonic (melody + accompaniment), balanced 4/8-bar phrases (question-answer), lighter texture, gradual crescendos, clear sonata form, elegant restraint prioritizing structure over emotion. Example: **Mozart - Symphony No. 40, 1st movement** – Crisp exposition introduces two contrasting themes, develops with punchy motifs, resolves neatly; transparent and witty.

### Early Romantic (1820–1850)

Lyrical, rubato (flexible tempo), richer harmonies, longer melodies expressing nature/heartache, some polyphony returns, more chromaticism (half-steps for tension). Bridges Classical clarity to excess. Example: **Schubert - Piano Sonata D. 960, 1st movement** – Flowing theme with sighs and pauses, develops introspectively; warmer and more song-like than Mozart.

### Mature Romantic (1850–1900)

Expressive extremes, huge dynamics/orchestras, lush chromatics/dissonance, rhythmic freedom, programmatic (story-painting), nationalism. Melody dominates for passion. Example: **Tchaikovsky - Symphony No. 6 "Pathétique", 4th movement** – Despairing march builds to wailing brass climaxes, fades tragically; bombastic and heart-wrenching.

### Modern (1900–1960)

Dissonant/atonal, irregular rhythms/meters, polytonality (multiple keys), percussive attacks, experimental forms, influenced by folk/jazz/industry. Raw power over beauty. Example: **Shostakovich - Symphony No. 5, 1st movement** – Jarring fanfares, grinding ostinatos, march-like menace; feels aggressive and unstable.

### Post-modern (1960+)

Minimalism (repetition/phasing), aleatory (chance), electronics, eclectic quotes; serene loops or chaotic collages. Reaction to Modern excess. Example: **Philip Glass - "Koyaanisqatsi" from soundtrack** – Arpeggios layer/accelerate hypnotically, evoking trance; no traditional melody, pure pattern buildup.

## Genre Differences

Genres vary by scale, intimacy, and role: solo vs. ensemble, abstract vs. narrative. Piano is agile; organ booms; chamber converses; orchestral overwhelms; opera dramatizes with voice.

### Piano/Harpsichord

Plucked (harpsichord: bright, no sustain) or hammered (piano: dynamic range). Intimate, virtuosic; Baroque ornate, Romantic poetic. Example: **Bach - Prelude in C Major, BWV 846 (harpsichord/piano)** – Flowing arpeggios over steady bass; crisp, non-legato texture vs. piano's warmer sustain.

### Organ

Sustained tones, massive registration (stops for power), pedal bass; church grandeur, improvisatory. Echoes like reverb doom. Example: **Buxtehude - Prelude and Fugue in C Major, BuxWV 137** – Thunderous pedals, swirling manuals; infinite sustain creates cavernous depth unlike piano's decay.

### Chamber

3–10 players (e.g., quartet: 2 violins/viola/cello); intimate dialogue, equal voices, conversational. Expressive nuance. Example: **Haydn - String Quartet Op. 76 No. 3 "Emperor"** – Instruments trade Emperor's Hymn theme playfully; tight interplay, no conductor dominance.

### Orchestral

Full ensemble (strings core + winds/brass/percussion); symphonies/concertos for epic scope, layered colors. Builds walls of sound. Example: **Beethoven - Symphony No. 5, 1st movement** – Fate motif pounds across sections; brass/winds explode in unison power absent in chamber.

### Opera

Sung drama: arias (solo showpiece), recitatives (speech-like), ensembles/chorus; orchestra supports narrative. Theatrical peaks. Example: **Verdi - "La donna è mobile" from Rigoletto** – Tenor aria with catchy tune over orchestral lilt; vocal fireworks + staging absent in pure instrumental.
